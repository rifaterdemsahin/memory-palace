uilding a memory palace in SketchUp is a fantastic way to bridge the gap between abstract imagination and concrete spatial memory. Since your brain is wired to remember 3D spaces much better than flat lists, "walking" through a model you built yourself can make your mnemonic anchors incredibly sticky.

1. How to Learn SketchUp for Memory Palaces
For this specific goal, you don't need to be an architect. You only need to learn the "Spatial Basics":

Step 1: The "Big Four" Tools: Master Line (L), Push/Pull (P), Move (M), and Orbit (O). These are enough to build basic rooms and corridors.

Step 2: Use the 3D Warehouse: Instead of modeling every chair or statue (your "loci"), download them for free from SketchUp's built-in 3D Warehouse. This saves hours of work.

Step 3: Organize with "Groups" and "Tags": This prevents your walls from "sticking" to your furniture, allowing you to move items around as you refine your palace.

Step 4: Set Up Scenes: Create "Scenes" (bookmarks) for each stop in your memory journey. You can then click through them to "auto-walk" through your palace for review.

Where to learn:

SketchUp Campus (Free): Their "SketchUp Fundamentals" track is the gold standard for beginners.

YouTube: Search for "SketchUp for Interior Design" to learn how to create rooms quickly.

2. The Cost (2026 Pricing)
SketchUp has moved entirely to a subscription model. For a memory palace project, you likely only need the Free or Go version.

Plan	Annual Cost	Best For...
SketchUp Free	$0	Hobbyists. Web-browser only. Good for basic 1-room palaces.
SketchUp Go	~$129/year	Modeling on an iPad. Great if you want to "hand-draw" your palace with an Apple Pencil.
SketchUp Pro	~$399/year	Heavy users. Includes the desktop app, better textures, and plugins.
Tip: Start with the Free version. It includes access to the 3D Warehouse, which is the most important feature for building palaces quickly.

3. Strategy for "Memory Architects"
When building your palace, don't make it too symmetrical. Our brains remember unique quirks. Add a weirdly shaped hallway or a bright red door in SketchUp to help that specific "station" stand out in your mind's eye.
